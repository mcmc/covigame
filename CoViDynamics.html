You're in charge!

Save your population and your economy!

Drag the magenta spot up and down to control how the virus rolls through the population.

Try to minimize deaths and minimize economic damage.

You are the leader of your community.
You can decide how much social isolation to enforce, whether to cancel events,
whether people should wear masks, whether they should shake hands, and so on.
All of this turns into a single number, the transmission factor, which you control.

You can decide whether to focus more on deaths or more on the economy.
Either way, as you practice, you will see why you need to get the transmission
factor down. Whether you do this through social distancing, contact tracing,
or other measures is up to you, and is not modeled by the game.

What is the purpose of this game? Do you not take it seriously?

We take it very seriously.
We have been shocked at the degree to which people (including ourselves)
did not understand much at all about the way social distancing measures do or don't help,
what herd immunity is and isn't, and what sort of future we can expect under various policy approaches.

The purpose of the game is to help ourselves and other people understand how these factors interact.

For example, if you get good at the game, you will see that your strategy does not depend much
at all on the exact parameters. So even though we don't have good data yet on the parameters
of this virus, we can still implement a close-to-optimal policy by carefully monitoring the
state of our medical system and adjusting the social distancing measures accordingly.

The Transmission Factor

The transmission factor is the average number of other people that an infected citizen
will transmit the virus to, before the infected citizen either (1) develops an immune response
and defeats the infection without showing symptoms, (2) develops symptoms and self-isolates
(for mild to "like-a-bad-flu" cases), or (3) develops symptoms and gets hospitalized (severe cases).

The transmission factor is the average for all people, including all those 3 types of cases.

If the virus is transmitted to somebody who already had the virus, the
target is considered to be immune, and does not become contagious again.

So if some fraction of the population is immune, then the virus will only be able to infect a fraction of
the people it is transmitted to. This is effectively like having a lower transmission factor. In the game, this
corresponds to how the green line maps to lower transmission factors (grey lines) as you move to the right.

What does the curved green line represent?

The curved green line is to help with the intuition that an epidemic is kind of like a ball rolling down a hill.
The steeper the hill, the faster the ball will speed up.
The ball is kind of like a bean-bag: If the hill is not steep enough, the ball will slow down and stop.

The green curve is steeper on the left, because the virus spreads more easily when nobody has caught the virus yet.
Above the yellow line, the curve is steep enough to make the ball roll faster.
Below the yellow line, the curve is not steep enough and the ball will slow down.

The height of the left-hand end of the curve, where the magenta dot is,
represents the transmission factor.
Really, it is not the height that matters, but the angle to the endpoint, as indicated by the grey lines.
The gray line you are currently at determines the rate at which the epidemic will grow or shrink.
Your position is shown by the little green dot under the rolling virus ball.

The "physics" of the ball follows "epidemic physics", which is slightly different from
"gravity physics", but still the intuition is close enough for it to be a useful analogy.
The difference is just that the speed of the ball is given by the vertical extent of the
"trail" of red people behind the ball, rather than by any physical "momentum", so the
speed of the ball can be changed at any time, just by changing the transmission factor.

What do the colors of people mean?

Purple people have not yet contracted the virus.
Red people contracted the virus recently and are contagious.
Blue people have recovered from the virus and are no longer contagious.
Note that red people may or may not show symptoms. Only a fraction of the red people need to be
hospitalized, and an even smaller fraction of them die. These numbers are shown only in the charts.

Epidemic vs. Pandemic

The coronavirus pandemic is a pandemic because it is spread over the world.
It consists of many many local epidemics. This game models such an epidemic.

When there are different sub-populations that have little interaction with each other
(and perhaps different social distancing behavior), then a more complex model is needed.
This is typically due to geographical/cultural separation, but can also correspond to different
subpopulations in a single society, if these subpopulations have limited contact with each other.

This game models a single population in which the individual
variation in behavior is effectively random across the population.

"Herd immunity" and the dotted yellow line

A dotted yellow line is visible above.
The farther the virus ball is above this region, the faster the epidemic will grow.
The farther the virus ball is below this region, the faster the epidemic will shrink.
If you are close to the line, the epidemic will stay about the same size.
(Staying the same size means it spreads to new people at the same rate that people recover at.)

When the transmission factor is normal (yes, many things can influence what you consider to be
"normal"), then herd immunity occurs at the point where the green line crosses the yellow line.

If a population has "herd immunity", it doesn't mean the people are all immune. People can still catch the disease.
It just means that any outbreaks that do occur will have a limited spread, and the outbreak will end itself naturally.
The higher the level of herd immunity, the quicker the flare-ups will die back down.

Herd immunity is not a strategy. It is a phenomenon.
(Just like the pandemic itself is not a strategy. It is a phenomenon.)
The point at which this phenomenon occurs depends on the transmission factor,
which depends both on properties of the virus and on societal behavior.
For example it will occur at a different point in a culture where people are very social and physically
close, than in a culture where people meet rarely and remain physically distant even when they meet.

Herd immunity (perhaps thanks to a vaccine) is the only way that an epidemic can end, unless
transmission-reduction measures are kept in place, such as social distancing or contact tracing.
Even then, you could argue that these measures simply make herd-immunity happen sooner.

(The epidemic can also end by eradicating the virus, but that is practically impossible
once an outbreak stops being contained, as is the case here. Even when there is a
vaccine, it is still very difficult. We have been trying to eradicate the poliovirus
for decades, but immunity is still achieved via herd immunity, via vaccines.)

In this game, herd immunity is the phenomenon that the virus ball slows down and
stops before hitting 100%, even when the magenta dot on the left is above the line.
